Problem

Tokenization modernizes settlement — but distribution still breaks on one question: who can hold the asset, and when?

Today: identity is siloed

  • Each issuer builds its own onboarding, eligibility rules, and operator workflows.
  • Investors repeat KYC/accreditation across tokens and venues.
  • Revocations, expiries, and sanctions don’t propagate cleanly.

Result: friction + risk

  • High onboarding cost, slow distribution, fragmented liquidity.
  • Inconsistent audit evidence across participants.
  • Operational burden lands on issuers and transfer agents — the least scalable place.
“

Act I–V

A five‑act story: from fragmentation to shared infrastructure.

Act I

Define eligibility as shared language

Shibui frames eligibility as claims (KYC, accreditation, residency, sanctions) with consistent meaning across tokens.

Act II

Separate trust policy from token logic

Issuers choose who they trust for each claim topic. Trust can evolve without rewriting token contracts.

Act III

Act V

Scale with standards, not bespoke integrations

Shibui aligns with token standards (ERC‑3643) and uses a verifiable credential substrate (EAS) — a pragmatic path for institutions.

ERC‑3643 semantics EAS attestations Pluggable verifiers

What this unlocks

  • Lower onboarding cost and faster distribution.
  • Consistent oversight via shared claim definitions and governance.
  • Composable market structure: multiple attesters, multiple venues, one language.

What Shibui is not

  • Not an identity monopoly — issuers choose trusted parties per topic.
  • Not a single vendor stack — interfaces are pluggable.
  • Not a “trust us” black box — attestations are verifiable and auditable.

The Ask

To make shared identity real, the market needs to converge on a small set of definitions and operating rules.

1) Adopt the language

Align on topic definitions (what “KYC” means, what “Accredited” means, what evidence is acceptable) so tokens verify consistently.

Outcome: interoperable eligibility

2) Participate as a trusted issuer

Banks, KYC providers, transfer agents, and intermediaries issue attestations under clear governance and audit expectations.

Outcome: portable credentials

3) Join the pilot

Launch a pilot with 1–2 claim topics (e.g., KYC + Accreditation), defined revocation semantics, and shared reporting.

Outcome: measurable time/cost reduction
Pilot success criteria
  • Onboarding time reduced by reusing attestations across at least two tokens or venues.
  • Revocation/expiry demonstrated with immediate enforcement.
  • Operator controls for trusted issuers per topic.
  • Audit‑ready evidence trail for compliance and oversight.

Specifications

A business‑first view: what’s standardized, what stays flexible, and how governance works.

Design principles

  • Small core: standardize only what must be shared.
  • Issuer control: each issuer defines trusted attesters per topic.
  • Portable credentials: investors reuse attestations across tokens.
  • Immediate enforcement: revocation and expiry are first‑class.
  • Auditability: claims are verifiable; policies are inspectable.

Schema cards

A compact mental model of the language.

Identity

How a wallet maps to a reusable identity (ownership + delegation).

  • Identity owner
  • Authorized agent / delegate
  • Wallet-to-identity resolution
Claim topic

A standard claim type the market can share (KYC, accreditation, residency…)

  • Topic id / name
  • Required evidence schema
  • Issuer policies
Attestation

A verifiable statement from a trusted issuer about an identity.

  • Issuer (attester)
  • Schema-conform data
  • Expiry + revocation
Trust policy

Issuer-by-issuer control over who is recognized for each topic.

  • Per-topic allowlist
  • Change management
  • Immediate effect on verification
Verification

Resolve identity → evaluate topics → return eligible / ineligible.

  • Missing / revoked / expired fails
  • Untrusted issuer fails
  • Multiple topics supported
Evidence trail

What compliance teams and supervisors can inspect.

  • Which topic(s) passed/failed
  • Which issuer was trusted
  • Timestamped revocations/expiries
Optional appendix: technical notes For engineers who want a minimal implementation view.

Minimal implementation (one possible path)

  • Use EAS as the attestation backend (issue/revoke/expire).
  • Keep ERC‑3643 claim semantics intact (topics define what must be checked).
  • Implement a pluggable verifier: resolve identity → validate attestations per topic.
  • Maintain per‑topic trusted issuer sets (configurable by the token issuer).
