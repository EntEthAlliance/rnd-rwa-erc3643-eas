Problem

Tokenization modernizes issuance and settlement, but distribution still breaks on one question: who can hold the asset, and when?

Today: eligibility is fragmented

  • Every issuer defines “KYC”, “accredited”, “qualified purchaser”, etc. differently.
  • Investors repeat the same checks across tokens and venues.
  • Updates (expiry, revocation, sanctions) are operationally heavy to apply consistently.

Result: cost + risk

  • Slower onboarding, slower distribution, higher servicing cost.
  • Inconsistent evidence across participants and jurisdictions.
  • Compliance posture depends on manual reconciliation.

Approach

Shibui focuses on a small, practical core: definitions, trusted issuers, and how change is handled.

Step 1

Standardize what “eligibility” means

Define common claim topics (e.g., KYC, residency, investor type) so different assets evaluate eligibility in the same way.

Shared definitions Comparable evidence Jurisdiction-aware
Step 2

Let issuers choose who they trust

Each asset can recognize a set of trusted issuers for each claim topic—without forcing a single vendor or monopoly.

Issuer control Multiple providers Clear governance
Step 3

Make updates predictable

Expiry and revocation are first‑class, so eligibility can change quickly across assets when risk changes.

Expiry Revocation Audit trail

What this enables

  • Lower onboarding cost and faster distribution.
  • More consistent controls across venues and issuers.
  • Better audit readiness with standard claim types and evidence expectations.

What Shibui is not

  • Not a single identity provider.
  • Not a required tech stack.
  • Not “trust us” compliance—definitions and policies are inspectable.

Details

A business‑first view of what’s standardized, what stays flexible, and where the technology fits.

What’s standardized

  • Claim topics (shared definitions for common eligibility requirements).
  • Evidence expectations (what is acceptable and comparable across participants).
  • Update semantics (expiry and revocation rules, plus an audit trail).

What stays flexible

  • Issuer choice: who you trust for each topic.
  • Implementation: integrate with your existing onboarding and compliance stack.
  • Operating model: governance can evolve as participation grows.

Standards alignment (high level)

Identity standard
Shibui defines claim topics, trust policies, and update semantics
Attestation backend
Ethereum Attestation Service (EAS) for issuance, expiry, and revocation
Token compliance semantics
Designed to interoperate with ERC‑3643-style eligibility checks

Goal: institutional compatibility — stable semantics, pluggable implementations.

Optional appendix: minimal technical notes Only if you want an implementation mental model.

Minimal implementation (one possible path)

  • Use EAS for attestations (issue/revoke/expire).
  • Use a standard set of claim topics to evaluate eligibility.
  • Let issuers configure trusted attesters per topic.
  • Expose an auditable result (which topics passed/failed and why).

This page is static and communication-first. The broader repo contains an MVP implementation for evaluation.

What to prove in a pilot

  1. Reuse a single eligibility credential across two assets or venues.
  2. Demonstrate expiry or revocation and show consistent enforcement.
  3. Confirm issuer control over which parties are trusted for each topic.
