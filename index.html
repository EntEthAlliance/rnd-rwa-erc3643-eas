The Medical Records Problem

Every hospital has its own patient file system. You get tests at Quest, an MRI at Mount Sinai, a specialist visit at Mayo. Each one verifies you're you. Each one stores results differently. When you show up somewhere new, you start over.

Now imagine a universal format. Each institution still owns their process, their standards, their liability. But the proof — "this patient was tested, here's the result, here's who verified it" — gets published in one format any hospital can read.

That's Shibui for tokenized finance.

Each one stores results in their own format. When you show up somewhere new, you start over — not because the tests weren't done, but because there's no shared language between systems.

Now imagine a universal medical record format. Quest still does the bloodwork. Mayo still does the diagnosis. Each institution owns their process, their standards, their liability. But the proof — "this patient was tested, here's the result, here's who verified it" — gets published in one format that any hospital can read.

That's Shibui. GLEIF does LEI verification. Your compliance team runs KYC. A regulator checks accreditation. Each entity owns its process entirely. Shibui doesn't replace any of them — it gives them a shared primitive to publish standardized, verifiable proof on-chain using EAS, which already runs on Base, Optimism, Arbitrum, and mainnet.

The result: an investor verified by Fidelity's KYC process carries that proof into a DTCC-issued token, a BlackRock fund, a tokenized treasury on Base — without re-verification, without new infrastructure, without anyone giving up control of their own compliance process.

One verification. One format. Every chain. Every token. Each institution sovereign.

DNS for Identity

Before DNS, visiting a website meant knowing the exact IP address. Every network had its own address book. Nothing was interoperable. The internet existed but couldn't scale because there was no shared naming layer.

DNS didn't replace anyone's servers or tell anyone what content to host. It just created one shared primitive: "this name resolves to this address, and here's who vouched for it."

Before DNS, if you wanted to visit a website you needed the exact IP address. Every network maintained its own address book. Nothing was interoperable. The internet existed but couldn't scale because there was no shared naming layer.

DNS didn't replace anyone's servers. It didn't tell anyone what content to host. It just created one shared primitive: "this name resolves to this address, and here's who vouched for it."

Shibui is DNS for institutional identity in tokenized markets. GLEIF still issues LEIs. Your compliance desk still runs KYC and AML. Regulators still set the rules. What Shibui adds is the shared publication layer — a way to take those verified facts and express them as on-chain attestations using EAS, a technical standard already live across Ethereum's major networks.

Three things that matter: every institution keeps full ownership of their identity and compliance process, the proof format is open and already adopted by Coinbase, Optimism, and the broader Ethereum ecosystem, and any tokenized asset on any supported chain can read that proof without custom integration.

Not a product. A primitive. The layer that makes institutional identity composable.
